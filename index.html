The Problem

You Can't Treat What You Can't Track

ALS is one of the most heterogeneous diseases in neurology. No two patients progress identically - yet the tools used to monitor them have changed little in decades. Clinicians rely on coarse, infrequent assessments that miss the week-to-week changes that define a patient's trajectory.

"The diagnostic delay is substantial in all groups of ALS." [2] Standard clinical tools like ALSFRS-R are sparsely sampled, subjective, and insensitive to short-term change. [6] The result: delayed diagnosis, poor trial stratification, and missed therapeutic windows.

By the time ALS is typically diagnosed, it has already caused significant and irreversible damage. Diagnosis occurs at Stage 2A - when the patient is already 35% through their disease course. [4] A monitoring gap this large has real consequences for patients, carers, and researchers trying to help them.

🔍

Differential Diagnosis

ALS shares features with PBP, PMA, and PLS. Without continuous biomarker data, distinguishing subtypes and confirming diagnosis is slow and invasive, involving needle EMG, nerve conduction studies, MRI, and muscle biopsy. [3]

💊

Drug Development

92.9% of Phase III ALS trials fail. Poor patient stratification and insensitive endpoints make it near-impossible to detect drug signal. EEG-derived biomarkers provide a continuous, objective measure where ALSFRS-R cannot. [8]

🏥

Ongoing Care

Patients attending specialist ALS clinics have a 43.7% reduced risk of death and 7.5 months additional survival versus general neurology. [9] Most patients see a specialist every 3-4 months - far too infrequently for a disease that changes week to week. [7]

Disease Context

Understanding ALS Progression

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis is a fatal neurodegenerative disease affecting upper and lower motor neurons. It is one of four major motor neuron diseases, with annual incidence of 5.5 per 100,000. [5] Average age at diagnosis is 55, and it is 20% more common in men. [5]

Clinical Staging (King's College System) [4]

  • 1 Symptom OnsetFirst region involvement. 80% of cases begin with weakness or spasticity, with muscle fasciculations.
  • 2A Diagnosis35% through disease course. Average diagnostic delay from symptom onset exceeds one year. [2]
  • 2B Second Region Involved38% through disease. Mobility, balance, and coordination begin to deteriorate.
  • 3 Third Region Involved61% through disease. 30% have swallowing difficulty; 40% reduced respiratory capacity; 50%+ require mobility aids.
  • 4A Gastrostomy Required77% through disease course. Nutritional support via feeding tube becomes necessary.
  • 4B Non-Invasive Ventilation80% through disease course. Respiratory failure managed with NIV; tracheostomy in late stages.
Over 55% of ALS patients experience mild to severe depression as measured by the Beck Depression Inventory, significantly correlated with ALSFRS-R score. [8] A decline of just 2 ALSFRS-R points in swallowing can be the difference between self-sufficiency and full caregiver dependence.

ALSFRS-R: The Current Gold Standard

The ALS Functional Rating Scale - Revised has been used since 1991 (last revised 2009). It measures 12 aspects of physical function across 4 domains: bulbar, fine motor, gross motor, and respiratory. Each scored 0-4; maximum total 48. [8]

In ALS clinical trials, patients decline approximately 1 point per month on average. [10] Yet ALSFRS-R is subjective, does not measure underlying pathophysiology, and is sampled only every 3-4 months in clinical practice - far too infrequently for the week-to-week changes patients experience. [7]

Our Platform

Making Neurodegeneration Measurable

The Neurodegenerative Monitoring Index (NMI) is a longitudinal, dry-EEG and multimodal platform that extracts clinically actionable biomarkers from within-patient brain-network change - at home, continuously.

Peer-Reviewed Evidence

The Only High-Frequency Longitudinal EEG Study in ALS

Our preliminary research - a 1.5-year, in-home longitudinal case study across two bulbar-onset ALS patients - is the first to validate resting-state EEG as a real-time gauge of neural integrity at the individual patient level with dense, repeated sampling. "Not one study has high frequency recording longitudinally in any ALS patients to directly validate the use in a real-time scenario." [11]

0.22+ deltaR2

EEG explains disease variance beyond time

EEG features accounted for up to 22.5% of additional variance in ALSFRS-R functional status beyond what simple temporal progression explains, with bootstrap confidence intervals excluding zero across all electrode montages. [11]

R=0.81

Single-electrode biomarkers track clinical decline

MSC theta global efficiency tracked ALSFRS-R with Spearman R = 0.81 after detrending (p = 3.9 x 10^-5). Alpha-power attenuation, aperiodic exponent shifts, and low-gamma power all showed robust longitudinal coupling. [11] Alpha reduction is the most consistent ALS biomarker in the literature. [12]

19 electrodes

Sparse electrodes are sufficient for home use

EEG-clinical associations were preserved across dense (124-channel) and sparse (19-electrode) montages. Consumer-grade dry-electrode headsets are viable for longitudinal neurodegeneration monitoring - consistent with findings in mild cognitive impairment. [11] Dry-electrode EEG is now reaching clinical-trial parity for biomarker tasks. [14]

18-25 sessions

Dense within-patient sampling is key

18-25 sessions per participant over months, compared to the field standard of 4-5 sessions with 5.4-month gaps. [13] High-frequency sampling unlocks the within-patient dynamics invisible to cross-sectional designs and sparse longitudinal cohorts.

Why EEG - Why Now

EEG is uniquely positioned for this problem

EEG has real limitations. But for longitudinal neurodegeneration monitoring, it offers a combination of properties that no other modality can match.

Motor and speech independent

Unlike EMG or speech analysis, resting-state EEG captures neural network changes even when motor or bulbar function is severely compromised - making it usable throughout all stages of ALS. [12]

Feasible at home

Dry-electrode EEG is reaching clinical parity for biomarker tasks with dramatically reduced setup burden. [14] Our data was collected entirely in patients' homes over 18 months. [11]

Captures what MRI cannot

Resting-state EEG networks known to be altered in ALS change on timescales [12] that only EEG can practically sample densely. MRI is too expensive, immobile, and infrequent.

Signal processing is the moat

Hardware commoditises. The value is in validated pipelines, patient-specific models, and the longitudinal data that trains them. EEG graph and connectivity metrics in ALS correlate with disease progression. [16]

UK SaMD regulation is tractable

MHRA guidance on Software as a Medical Device is clearer than ever. A regulated route for an EEG-AI monitoring product is achievable with the right clinical evidence package. [17]

A platform, not a point solution

The same framework - longitudinal neural biomarkers - extends beyond ALS to Parkinson's, MCI, and other neurodegenerative conditions. The first indication de-risks the platform and builds the proprietary dataset.

Platform Thesis

Large-Scale Brain Change is Trackable

Our core thesis is simple: any disease that produces significant, sustained changes in large-scale brain network organisation should leave a measurable signature in longitudinal EEG. We have begun validating this in ALS. The same framework extends to a much broader set of conditions.

We are not proposing EEG solves everything. We are proposing that a validated, within-patient, longitudinal EEG signal - combined with multimodal data - provides a practical and scalable window into neural health that current clinical tools cannot.

The thesis: If a disease meaningfully disrupts cortical network topology, spectral dynamics, or large-scale connectivity over months - resting-state EEG, sampled densely and analysed within-patient, can serve as a continuous, objective gauge of that disruption. We start where the evidence is strongest, and build outward.

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis

We have direct preliminary evidence. In-home longitudinal rsEEG tracks ALSFRS-R functional status within individual patients across 18-25 sessions over 1.5 years. Alpha attenuation, theta decoupling, and aperiodic exponent shifts correlate robustly with clinical decline. [11]

Current Focus

Stroke

Post-stroke neural recovery monitoring

Stroke produces acute and evolving large-scale network disruption that is well-suited to longitudinal EEG tracking. Recovery trajectories vary significantly between patients, making within-patient monitoring particularly valuable for rehabilitation planning and clinical trials. We have preliminary evidence supporting EEG-detectable network changes in stroke recovery.

Mid-Term

Alzheimer's Disease

Including MCI and prodromal stages

A substantial body of EEG biomarker literature already exists. Slowing of dominant frequency, reduced alpha and beta power, and altered connectivity are well-documented. Longitudinal within-patient validation using our framework is the natural next step.

Mid-Term

Parkinson's Disease

Including prodromal monitoring

EEG beta-band hypersynchrony in sensorimotor networks and slowing of peak frequency are established Parkinson's signatures. Longitudinal monitoring of neural network state complements existing wearable motor assessments and opens a non-invasive window into dopaminergic system decline.

Mid-Term

Frontotemporal Dementia

FTD and ALS-FTD continuum

FTD overlaps with ALS in up to 15% of cases. EEG changes in frontotemporal networks are documented, and our ALS work makes this a natural adjacency - same platform, same electrode configurations, closely related pathophysiology.

The evidence bar under ALS and Stroke reflects NMI-specific preliminary data. Mid-term conditions are included on the basis of existing peer-reviewed EEG literature and biological plausibility. Prioritisation reflects strength of evidence, unmet clinical need, and feasibility of longitudinal within-patient study design.
